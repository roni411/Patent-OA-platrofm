# Legal References — Patent Examiner Skill

## EP / EPO

### Guidelines for Examination (key chapters)
- **G-VI (Novelty):** Definition of prior art, what counts as "directly and unambiguously derivable"
- **G-VII (Inventive Step):** Problem-Solution Approach (PSA) — mandatory framework at EPO
  - Step 1: Identify closest prior art
  - Step 2: Determine objective technical problem
  - Step 3: Would skilled person have arrived at claimed invention?
- **F-IV (Clarity, Art. 84):** Claim must be clear and supported by description

### Key EPO Case Law
- **T 0002/83** (Simethicone tablet) — Problem-Solution Approach established
- **T 0641/00** (COMVIK) — Technical character required for inventive step
- **T 1989/14** — "Implicit disclosure" must be "necessarily" present, not merely possible
- **G 2/98** — Same invention standard for priority
- **T 0190/99** — Closest prior art selection principles

### Useful phrases (EPO)
- "The cited document does not directly and unambiguously disclose..."
- "The Examiner's reading of [document] goes beyond what is explicitly or implicitly disclosed therein..."
- "The objective technical problem can be formulated as..."
- "The skilled person would not have had a reasonable expectation of success..."

## US / USPTO

### MPEP Key Sections
- **MPEP 2131** — Anticipation (§102) — all elements in single reference
- **MPEP 2141** — Obviousness (§103) — Graham v. John Deere factors
- **MPEP 2143** — Exemplary rationales for combining references (KSR)
- **MPEP 2161** — Written description (§112 first paragraph)
- **MPEP 2164** — Enablement (§112 first paragraph)
- **MPEP 2173** — Definiteness (§112 second paragraph)

### Key Case Law
- **Graham v. John Deere (1966)** — Four-factor obviousness test
- **KSR Int'l v. Teleflex (2007)** — Flexible obviousness standard, "obvious to try"
- **In re Kubin (2009)** — "Obvious to try" with finite predictable solutions
- **Ariad Pharmaceuticals v. Eli Lilly (2010)** — Written description standard

### Useful phrases (USPTO)
- "The Examiner's assertion that [reference] discloses [element] is respectfully contested..."
- "The Examiner has not provided a sufficient rationale for combining [Ref A] with [Ref B]..."
- "No motivation to combine has been articulated..."
- "The proposed combination would render [Ref A] unsatisfactory for its intended purpose..."

### §103 Checklist
When arguing against obviousness:
- [ ] Did Examiner identify primary reference?
- [ ] Did Examiner identify motivation to combine?
- [ ] Is the combination technically feasible?
- [ ] Would combination destroy the principle of operation of primary reference?
- [ ] Are there objective indicia of non-obviousness (commercial success, long-felt need, teaching away)?

## CN / CNIPA

### Patent Law & Implementing Regulations
- **Art. 22** — Novelty and Inventive Step requirements
- **Art. 22(3)** — Inventive step: prominent substantive features + notable progress
- **Art. 26.4** — Clarity and support (frequently cited)
- **Rule 20** — Unity of invention

### Examination Guidelines (审查指南) Key Points
- Novelty: similar to EPO — direct and unambiguous disclosure
- Inventive Step: "three-step method" similar to EPO's PSA
  1. Determine closest prior art
  2. Determine distinguishing features and the technical effect
  3. Determine whether skilled person would arrive at invention
- Art. 26.4 rejections: often broad — argue the specific claim language is supported by description

### Useful phrases (CN responses, in English)
- "Applicant respectfully submits that the cited reference does not disclose the feature of..."
- "The distinguishing feature of Claim [X] resides in..."
- "The cited document fails to suggest or motivate..."

## JP / JPO

### Patent Act Key Articles
- **Art. 29(1)** — Novelty
- **Art. 29(2)** — Inventive Step ("a person ordinarily skilled in the art could have easily made")
- **Art. 36(6)(i)** — Support requirement
- **Art. 36(6)(ii)** — Clarity

### JPO Examination Guidelines Key Points
- Inventive Step: similar to EPO but "could have easily arrived at" is the standard
- Examiner typically provides detailed mapping — respond to each point individually
- Interview (面接審査): Highly recommended when there is technical misunderstanding
  - Can be requested by applicant or suggested by examiner
  - Effective for clearing up claim interpretation issues

### Useful phrases (JP responses)
- "Applicant respectfully submits that the Examiner has misread the cited document..."
- "The feature recited in Claim [X] differs from the disclosure of [Document] in that..."
- "There is no teaching or suggestion in the cited art to..."
